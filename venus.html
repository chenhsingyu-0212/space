Introduction

Venus is the second planet of the eight planets in the solar system, orbiting at a distance of approximately 0.72 astronomical units from the Sun, positioned between Mercury and Earth. A single day on Venus is equivalent to 243 Earth days, and its magnetic field is only about one one-hundred-thousandth as strong as Earth’s. Uniquely, Venus rotates in the opposite direction to Earth and most other planets, making it stand out as one of the solar system's more peculiar planets.

Surface Features of Venus

Venus is shrouded by a thick yellow atmosphere that makes it impossible to observe the surface through a telescope. In 1975, the Soviet Union launched the Venera 9 and Venera 10 missions, which successfully landed on Venus and captured images of the planet’s surface. These images revealed that Venus' surface is primarily composed of basaltic rock, with boulders ranging in size from 30 centimeters to 1 meter.

Environmental Conditions on Venus

Venus’ thick carbon dioxide atmosphere leads to an intense greenhouse effect, resulting in surface temperatures reaching an extreme 470°C, creating a hellish environment. The atmospheric pressure on the surface is 90 times that of Earth. Under these extreme conditions, liquid water cannot exist, making Venus inhospitable for life as we know it. The surface gravity on Venus is about 0.91 times that of Earth's, fairly similar to our planet.

Internal Structure of Venus

Scientists believe that Venus’ internal structure is similar to Earth's. Venus likely has a mantle composed mainly of silicate minerals like olivine and pyroxene, a silicate crust, and an iron-nickel core. Its average density is 5.24 g/cm³, making it the third densest planet in the solar system, after Earth and Mercury.
